N-Body Gravitational Simulation

This project simulates the motion of multiple celestial bodies under Newtonian gravity. It models solar system–like dynamics using Newton’s law of gravitation and a numerical integrator (Runge–Kutta 4) to solve the resulting coupled differential equations.

1 · Newton’s Law of Gravitation

The gravitational force between two point masses is given by: \[ \mathbf{F}_{ij} = -G \frac{m_i m_j}{r_{ij}^3} (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j) \] where \( G \) is the gravitational constant, \( m_i \) and \( m_j \) are the masses, and \( \mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j \) is their separation vector.

2 · Equations of Motion

Each body experiences the net force from all other bodies, resulting in the coupled ODEs: \[ \frac{d^2 \mathbf{r}_i}{dt^2} = \sum_{j \neq i} -G \frac{m_j}{|\mathbf{r}_j - \mathbf{r}_i|^3} (\mathbf{r}_i - \mathbf{r}_j) \] These are numerically solved for \( \mathbf{r}_i(t) \) using RK4 integration.

3 · Units and Assumptions

Units are chosen so that \( G = 1 \), distances are in astronomical units (AU), masses are in solar masses, and time is in years. The simulation assumes no relativistic effects and ignores collisions — all bodies are point masses.

4 · Numerical Method

The simulation uses the 4th-order Runge–Kutta method to evolve the system. RK4 balances speed and stability, making it ideal for moderate-accuracy orbital modeling.

5 · Visualizing the Trajectories

Each body’s position is tracked over time and plotted to show its orbital path. For small systems, this reveals familiar patterns like ellipses and loops. Complex interactions (like perturbations) can be seen in multi-body cases.
