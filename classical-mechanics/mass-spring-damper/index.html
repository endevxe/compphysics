Mass–Spring–Damper Oscillator

Many mechanical and control‑system problems reduce to a single degree of freedom mass \( m \) attached to a spring of stiffness \( k \) and a damper with coefficient \( c \). The governing differential equation is \[ m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+k x = 0, \] where \( x(t) \) is the displacement from equilibrium. The term \( kx \) pulls the mass back to equilibrium, while \( c\dot{x} \) removes energy and models friction or fluid damping.

1 · Damping Ratio and Natural Frequency

Dividing by the mass puts the equation in standard form \[ \ddot{x}+2\zeta\omega_n\dot{x}+\omega_n^{2}x = 0, \qquad \omega_n=\sqrt{\dfrac{k}{m}},\quad \zeta=\dfrac{c}{2\sqrt{km}}. \]

The dimensionless damping ratio \( \zeta \) determines the qualitative behavior:  \( \zeta<1 \) gives underdamped oscillations, \( \zeta=1 \) is critical damping, and \( \zeta>1 \) is overdamped.

2 · Analytic Underdamped Solution

When \( \zeta<1 \) the solution is an exponentially decaying cosine: \[ x(t)=e^{-\zeta\omega_n t}\! \Bigl(A\cos\omega_d t + B\sin\omega_d t\Bigr),\quad \omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-\zeta^{2}}. \] Constants \( A \) and \( B \) come from the initial displacement and velocity. Critical and overdamped cases have purely exponential (non‑oscillatory) responses.

3 · Numerical Integration with RK4

Even though an analytic solution exists, a Runge–Kutta integrator is included to demonstrate how one would treat a more complicated or non‑linear variant. The second‑order ODE is rewritten as two coupled first‑order equations for \( x \) and \( v=\dot{x} \) and advanced with the classical fourth‑order RK4 scheme.

4 · Interpreting the Plot

The script plots both the analytic solution (for the underdamped case) and the RK4 numerical result. For appropriate step size \( \Delta t \) the two curves coincide, illustrating the accuracy of RK4. Changing the damping coefficient in the code lets you visualise overdamped and critically damped behavior as well.
