Projectile Motion with Quadratic Drag

Real projectiles do not follow perfect parabolas because air resistance acts opposite to the velocity. A common empirical model assumes a drag force proportional to the square of the speed, \[ \mathbf{F}_{\text{drag}} = -k\,v\,\mathbf{v}, \] where \( k \) depends on shape, cross‑sectional area, and air density. For small objects at modest speeds this “quadratic drag” captures the dominant dissipative effect.

1 · Equations of Motion

Resolving forces horizontally and vertically for a projectile of mass \( m \) gives \[ \frac{dv_x}{dt} = -\frac{k}{m}\,v\,v_x, \qquad \frac{dv_y}{dt} = -g -\frac{k}{m}\,v\,v_y, \] with \( v=\sqrt{v_x^{2}+v_y^{2}} \). Integrating these together with \( \frac{dx}{dt}=v_x \) and \( \frac{dy}{dt}=v_y \) forms a set of four first‑order coupled ODEs. Setting \( k=0 \) recovers the ideal, drag‑free parabola \( y = x\tan\theta - \frac{gx^{2}}{2v_0^{2}\cos^{2}\theta} \).

2 · Numerical Integration

The code integrates both models using the classical fourth‑order Runge–Kutta step. Identical initial speed \( v_0 \) and launch angle \( \theta \) are used; only the drag coefficient \( k \) is varied. Because the system is theoretically defined once \( g, k, m, v_0, \theta \) are chosen, no experimental data are required to plot and compare trajectories.

3 · Interpreting the Plot

The ideal path (no drag) is a perfect parabola and lands farthest down‑range. The drag‑affected path is flatter and lands sooner because kinetic energy is continually removed by the air. The stronger the coefficient \( k \), the shorter and lower the real trajectory becomes.
