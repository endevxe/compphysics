Monte Carlo Error Propagation

1. Motivation

When quantities with uncertainties are combined mathematically, the resulting uncertainty in the final value can be difficult to estimate analytically — especially for nonlinear formulas. Monte Carlo methods make this easy by simulating many possible outcomes based on the distributions of input variables.

2. Example Problem

Suppose we want to calculate the area \( A = L \times W \) of a rectangle. If we know:

  • Length: \( L = 10.0 \pm 0.5 \ \text{cm} \)
  • Width: \( W = 4.0 \pm 0.2 \ \text{cm} \)

We sample \( L \) and \( W \) values from normal distributions and compute \( A \) for each sample. After thousands of trials, the standard deviation of the results gives the uncertainty in area.

3. How It Works

For each trial:

  1. Randomly pick a length \( L_i \sim \mathcal{N}(10.0, 0.5) \)
  2. Randomly pick a width \( W_i \sim \mathcal{N}(4.0, 0.2) \)
  3. Calculate area: \( A_i = L_i \times W_i \)
The full set \( \{A_i\} \) approximates the distribution of the true area.

5. Applications

Monte Carlo error propagation is used in physics experiments, lab analysis, and any scientific calculation where complex dependencies or nonlinear relationships make analytical uncertainty propagation impractical.
