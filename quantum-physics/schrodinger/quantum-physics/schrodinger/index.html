Time-Independent Schrödinger Equation

This simulation numerically solves the Schrödinger equation for a quantum particle in a one-dimensional potential well (infinite square well).

1. Introduction

The Schrödinger equation is a fundamental equation of quantum mechanics. It describes how the quantum state of a physical system evolves. In the time-independent form, it determines the stationary states and allowed energy levels of a particle:

$$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} + V(x)\psi(x) = E\psi(x) $$

2. Infinite Potential Well (Particle in a Box)

For a particle confined in a 1D box of length \( L \), with infinitely high potential walls:

  • \( V(x) = 0 \) for \( 0 < x < L \)
  • \( V(x) = \infty \) otherwise

Inside the box, the equation simplifies to:

$$ \frac{d^2\psi(x)}{dx^2} + k^2 \psi(x) = 0,\quad \text{where } k^2 = \frac{2mE}{\hbar^2} $$

3. Boundary Conditions and Solution

The wavefunction must vanish at the walls: \( \psi(0) = \psi(L) = 0 \). Solving this boundary value problem gives:

$$ \psi_n(x) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{L}} \sin\left( \frac{n\pi x}{L} \right), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \dots $$ $$ E_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2 \hbar^2}{2mL^2} $$

These are the quantized energy levels and normalized wavefunctions.

4. Numerical Simulation

In the simulation, we discretize space and solve the Schrödinger equation using a finite difference method (such as the shooting method or matrix diagonalization). This gives us the wavefunctions and energy levels numerically.

5. Why No Real Data is Needed

This is a theoretical model. By assigning values like \( \hbar = 1 \), \( m = 1 \), and \( L = 1 \), we simulate the behavior of a generic quantum particle. The results are scaled and apply to ideal systems — no experimental data is needed to compute eigenstates or visualize them.
