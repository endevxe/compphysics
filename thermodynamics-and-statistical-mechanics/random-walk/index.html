Brownian Motion, Random Walk & Diffusion

The erratic motion of pollen grains observed by Robert Brown is now understood as the cumulative effect of countless molecular collisions. In statistical mechanics this is idealised as a random walk: at each time step a particle makes an independent, random step of fixed length.

1 · Lattice Random Walk Model

In two dimensions we let the walker start at the origin and move one lattice spacing in one of four cardinal directions with equal probability. Repeating this for many walkers produces an ensemble suitable for averaging.

2 · Mean‑Square Displacement

A hallmark of diffusion is that the mean‑square displacement (MSD) grows linearly with time: \[ \langle r^{2}(t) \rangle = 4 D t \quad\text{(in 2‑D)} , \] where \( D \) is the diffusion coefficient. By fitting a straight line to the simulated MSD curve we can derive \( D \) directly from the random walk data, without any external measurement.

3 · Monte‑Carlo Simulation

The script initialises thousands of independent walkers, advances each one in parallel, and records \( \langle r^{2} \rangle \) after every step. A simple linear regression on the MSD versus step number yields the slope and therefore the diffusion coefficient via \( D = \text{slope}/4 \).

4 · Results & Interpretation

At long times the MSD points fall neatly on a straight line, confirming diffusive behaviour. Deviations at early times reflect the discrete lattice nature and finite sampling. Increasing the number of walkers reduces statistical noise; increasing the number of steps extends the range over which the linear fit is valid.
